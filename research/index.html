National Identity and Immigration Attitudes

There are two competing hypotheses about the impact of national identity on attitudes towards immigration. The first, based on research in Europe and in the U.S., is that national identities act as exclusionary forces: they make people more opposed to immigration. This means, for instance, that making salient the national identity of individuals will increase their opposition to immigration. The second hypothesis is that a national identity can be made to be inclusive if immigration is built into one of its founding elements.

Using Canada as a case, I stack these two competing hypotheses against each other and test the possibility that some national identities might represent an inclusive force. Part of this research was funded by the Environics Institute.

Social Desirability Bias as an Object of Research
